Yvonne Brill

Brilliant rocket scientist, mother, and wife.

  • Born in Winnepeg, Manitoba, Canada on December 30, 1924 to Belgian immigrants
  • Studied chemistry and mathematics at the University of Manitoba, unable to study engineering due to her gender
  • Patented the hydrazine resistojet, a new rocket engine which became the new industry standard because it performed better, more reliably, and used less fuel
  • Contributed to TIROS, the first weather satellite capable of remote sensing the Earth, used for weather observation
  • Contributed to Nova, a series of rocket designs proposed for the American moon missions
  • Contributed to Explorer 32, a satellite used to study the structure and physics of the Earth's upper atmosphere
  • Contributed to the Mars Observer spacecraft, a robotic space probe launched to study the Martian surface, atmosphere, climate, and magnetic field
  • Received many awards an honors in her field, such as the AIAA Wyld Propulsion Award and the American Association of Engineering Societies John Fritz Medal
  • Received an award for successfully returning to her career after taking 8 years off for her 3 children.
  • Elected to the National Academy of Engineering
  • Received the National Medal of Technology and Innovation from Barack Obama

    • Brill passed away on March 27, 2013, after complications due to breast cancer. The obituary the New York Times was originally to publish began "She made a mean beef stroganoff, followed her husband from job to job and took eight years off from work to raise three children." This was heavily criticized as an overemphasis on family life and a diminishment of her scientific career.
